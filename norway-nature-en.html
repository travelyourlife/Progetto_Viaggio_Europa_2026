🌿 Nature and Wildlife: Norway

Encyclopaedic guide to Norwegian nature — the spectacular Arctic biodiversity, the ancient fjords, the Lofoten Islands, and marine ecosystems.

Norway offers some of the most dramatic and pristine natural landscapes on the planet. From the abyssal depths of the fjords to the rugged peaks of the Lofoten Islands, the Gulf Stream moderates a climate that would otherwise be inhospitable, allowing the existence of unique ecosystems and a rich Arctic and subarctic fauna.

🌲 Flora: From the Boreal Forest to the Arctic Tundra

Norwegian vegetation is strongly influenced by altitude and latitude. Due to the harsh climate, only 3% of the land is arable, while the rest is divided between forests, mountains, and tundra.

The Dominant Tree Species

As in much of Scandinavia, the boreal forest covers the inland areas, but the coast and plateau feature unique adaptations.

Downy Birch and Mountain Birch (Betula pubescens, in Norwegian: dunbjørk / fjellbjørk) Birch is the most widespread deciduous tree. The mountain subspecies (fjellbjørk) forms the upper tree line on the Scandinavian mountains. Unlike the straight birches of the lowlands, these take on twisted, knotted, and low forms to withstand icy winds and the weight of snow. Their bright green foliage in summer turns into a spectacular golden yellow in autumn.
Scots Pine (Pinus sylvestris, furu) A majestic tree that dominates the drier, rockier forests. In the inland valleys, pines can reach 30 metres in height and live for centuries. Their resinous wood, naturally resistant to decay, has been used for millennia to build the famous Stavkirke (wooden churches) and Viking ships.
Norway Spruce (Picea abies, gran) Dominates the moist and fertile inland soils. It forms dense, dark forests, the classic habitat of trolls in Norwegian fairy tales. It is not native to the west coast (the fjord region), where it has only been widely planted in the last century for the timber industry.

The Treasure of the Tundra and Underbrush

The right of public access to nature (Allemannsretten) allows the gathering of wild fruits, an activity deeply rooted in Norwegian culture.

Cloudberry (Rubus chamaemorus, multe) Known as the "gold of the Arctic," it is Norway’s most precious and sought-after berry. It grows exclusively in wet and boggy peatlands. The white flower turns into a fruit resembling a raspberry that changes from bright red to golden orange when ripe (July–August). Rich in vitamin C, it has a tart and honeyed flavour. Traditionally served at Christmas as multekrem (with whipped cream).
Lingonberry (Vaccinium vitis-idaea, tyttebær) Small red berries that grow in dense evergreen carpets in pine forests. Being naturally rich in benzoic acid (a natural preservative), they can be preserved for months simply crushed in their own juice. Tyttebær jam is the indispensable accompaniment to meatballs and game.
Chanterelle (Cantharellus cibarius, kantarell) The most beloved mushroom among Norwegian foragers. It appears between August and September in mixed forests, often near birches and spruces. Its unmistakable egg-yolk yellow colour and fruity aroma make it easy to identify (see identification table in the Finland section).
🦌 Fauna: The Giants of the North and the Lords of the Sky

Norway is home to some of the most spectacular wildlife populations in Europe, with a clear division between the inland terrestrial fauna and the rich marine life along the coast.

Terrestrial Mammals

Musk Ox (Ovibos moschatus, moskus) A living relic of the Ice Age. Although it became extinct in Europe thousands of years ago, it was successfully reintroduced to Dovrefjell National Park in the 1930s. It can weigh up to 400 kg and has an exceptionally insulating double-layer coat (the qiviut). Despite its bulky build, it can charge at 60 km/h. Observing it requires caution: you must keep at least 200 metres distance.
Moose (Alces alces, elg) The "King of the Forest". It is the largest cervid in the world, with males exceeding 700 kg and possessing palmate antlers up to 2 metres wide. There are about 100,000 moose in Norway. They are solitary animals that prefer swampy forests and young birch woods. Be careful when driving at dawn and dusk: moose-related road accidents are a real hazard.
Reindeer (Rangifer tarandus, reinsdyr) In the Arctic regions (Finnmark, Troms, and part of Nordland), reindeer are semi-domesticated, owned by the indigenous Sámi people. They roam freely between winter pastures (inland) and summer pastures (coast and islands). The Svalbard archipelago is home to a smaller, stockier endemic subspecies, the Svalbard reindeer.

The Wealth of the Sea and Skies

White-tailed Sea Eagle (Haliaeetus albicilla, havørn) The largest bird of prey in Northern Europe, with a wingspan that can reach 2.45 metres. The Lofoten Islands host the highest density of sea eagles in the world. They can be seen majestically soaring above the fjords, using updrafts before diving to snatch fish from the surface with their powerful talons.
Atlantic Puffin (Fratercula arctica, lundefugl) The "clown of the sea", recognisable by its triangular, brightly coloured beak. It spends the winter out at sea and returns to land only in summer to breed. The island of Røst, at the southern tip of the Lofoten, hosts the largest colony in Norway (about 25% of the national population). Unfortunately, the species is in decline due to warming waters that drive away the small fish they feed on.
Sperm Whale (Physeter macrocephalus, spermasetthval) The largest toothed predator on Earth. Adult males, up to 20 metres long, gather off Andenes (Vesterålen) year-round. This area is unique because the continental shelf drops steeply near the coast, creating the perfect habitat for giant squids, the sperm whale’s prey, which it hunts by diving to depths of up to 2,000 metres for over an hour.
Orca (Orcinus orca, spekkhogger) Every winter (from November to February), hundreds of orcas and humpback whales follow the vast herring shoals in the northern fjords (often near Tromsø and the Lofoten). They hunt in groups using a technique called the "carousel": they encircle the herring, drive them to the surface, and stun them with powerful tail slaps.
🪨 Geology: The Cradle of the Fjords

The Norwegian landscape is an open book of geological history, sculpted by the violence of ice and the uplift of the Earth's crust.

The Baltic Craton and the Oldest Rocks in Europe

Norway largely rests on the Baltic Craton (or Baltic Shield), a piece of primordial Earth's crust. In the Lofoten and Vesterålen islands, the exposed rock (gneiss and granite) is an astonishing around 3 billion years old, making it one of the oldest rock formations in Europe. This crustal stability has allowed the rocks to survive countless geological eras.

The Formation of the Fjords

Fjords were not carved by rivers, but by glaciers. During the last ice ages, the whole of Scandinavia was covered by an ice sheet up to 3 km thick.

  1. The weight and movement of the glaciers carved deep "U"-shaped valleys into the hard rock.
  2. Because solid ice can erode even below sea level, these valleys were worn to incredible depths (the Sognefjord is 1,308 metres deep).
  3. When the glaciers retreated (around 10,000 years ago), seawater flooded the valleys.
  4. A typical feature is the "threshold" at the fjord’s mouth: the seabed is shallower where the fjord meets the open sea, because there the glacier floated and stopped eroding the seabed, depositing debris (moraines).

The Caledonian Orogeny

About 425 million years ago, the continental plate of Greenland/North America collided with Europe. The titanic impact uplifted the Caledonian mountain range, mountains that at the time were as high as today’s Himalayas (up to 10,000 metres). Erosion over hundreds of millions of years has reduced them to the present-day Scandinavian Alps (whose highest peak, Galdhøpiggen, now reaches 2,469 metres).

🌌 Natural Phenomena: Extreme Lights

Being crossed by the Arctic Circle (66°33' N), Norway experiences extreme light conditions that dictate the rhythms of nature and human life.

The Midnight Sun

North of the Arctic Circle, in summer, the tilt of the Earth's axis causes the sun to never fully set. In the Lofoten Islands (68° N), the sun remains constantly above the horizon from 26 May to 17 July (about 52 consecutive days). This phenomenon stimulates explosive plant growth and allows seabirds to hunt 24 hours a day to feed their chicks.

The Maelstrom (Moskstraumen)

One of the most powerful and dangerous tidal phenomena in the world. It occurs between the southern tip of the Lofoten (Moskenesøya) and the isolated islet of Mosken. Unlike most whirlpools that form in narrow channels, the Moskstraumen forms in open sea. It is caused by the enormous mass of water pushed back and forth by the tides between the Vestfjord and the Norwegian Sea, colliding with a complex underwater topography. It inspired Edgar Allan Poe and Jules Verne.

🌳 Ecosystems: The Invisible Forests

Beneath the icy surface of the fjords and along the rugged coastline lie ecosystems as rich as those on land.

Kelp Forests (Laminaria)

The cold coastal waters of Norway host vast underwater "forests" of giant brown algae (kelp). These forests are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth and serve as vital nurseries for countless fish species, including the famous Norwegian Arctic cod (Skrei). In the 1970s, an ecological imbalance (possibly linked to overfishing of predators) caused a population explosion of green sea urchins, which devoured and destroyed over 2,000 km² of kelp forests. Today, large-scale restoration projects are slowly bringing this crucial habitat back to life.

Cold Water Coral Reefs

Not all corals live in the tropics. The Norwegian fjords and continental shelf host some of the largest cold water coral reefs in the world, mainly formed by the species Lophelia pertusa. They grow in total darkness, between 40 and 1,000 metres deep, feeding on plankton carried by strong currents. The Røst Reef, located off the Lofoten Islands, is the largest known deep-water coral reef in the world: 43 km long and nearly 7 km wide.
