## FLORA — DETAILED

### Trees (31 tree species in Finland)
- **Pino silvestre** (Scots pine, Pinus sylvestris, FI: mänty) — 41% of growing stock. Dominates dry/sandy soils. Reaches 20-30m. Bark orange-brown upper trunk. Needles in pairs, 4-7cm. Cones 3-7cm. Pioneer after fire. Extends to extreme north. National tree of Finland.
- **Abete rosso** (Norway spruce, Picea abies, FI: kuusi) — 38% of growing stock. Climax species on fertile soils. 20-40m tall. Conical shape. Needles single, 1-2.5cm. Cones 10-18cm (largest Finnish cones). Dominates without fire. Heavy humus layer underneath.
- **Betulla bianca/argentata** (Silver birch, Betula pendula, FI: rauduskoivu) — Pioneer species. 15-25m. White peeling bark. Triangular leaves with serrated edges. Sap harvested in spring (koivunmahla). Wood used for sauna whisks (vihta/vasta).
- **Betulla pubescente** (Downy birch, Betula pubescens, FI: hieskoivu) — Tolerates wetter soils than silver birch. Softer leaves, hairy twigs.
- **Pioppo tremulo** (Aspen, Populus tremula, FI: haapa) — Important for biodiversity, hosts many red-listed species. Leaves tremble in wind.
- **Ontano grigio** (Grey alder, Alnus incana, FI: harmaaleppä) — Fixes nitrogen. Pioneer on fertile soils after fire/clearing.
- **Ontano nero** (Black alder, Alnus glutinosa, FI: tervaleppä) — Wetland species, along streams.
- **Sorbo** (Rowan/Mountain ash, Sorbus aucuparia, FI: pihlaja) — Red berries in autumn, important bird food.
- **Salice** (Willow spp., Salix, FI: paju) — Multiple species, wetlands and lakeshores.
- **Betulla nana** (Dwarf birch, Betula nana, FI: vaivaiskoivu) — Arctic/alpine, max 1m tall, Lapland fells.

### Forest composition (8th National Forest Inventory):
- Pine 41%, Spruce 38%, Broad-leaved 21%
- Total forest species: 31 trees, 350 vascular plants, 400 cryptogams, 5000 fungi, 11000 invertebrates, 76 birds, 36 mammals
- 75% of Finland is forested (22.8 million ha)
- Fire cycle historically 46-122 years depending on soil type
- Without fire, spruce dominates all mineral soils eventually

### Berries (Everyman's Rights — Jokamiehenoikeus)
- **Mirtillo selvatico** (Bilberry, Vaccinium myrtillus, FI: mustikka) — Jul-Sep. Blue-black. Forests and bogs. Rich in antioxidants, vitamin C. Stains fingers purple. Mustikkapiirakka (bilberry pie) is classic. Leaves turn red in autumn (ruska).
- **Mirtillo rosso** (Lingonberry, Vaccinium vitis-idaea, FI: puolukka) — Aug-Oct. Red, plump, dry. Canopy forests. Used in jams, juices, pies. Easy to pick, doesn't stain.
- **Camemoro** (Cloudberry, Rubus chamaemorus, FI: lakka/hilla) — Jul-Aug. Orange when ripe, red=unripe. Bogs and marshes. "Gold of the bogs". Served with bread cheese (leipäjuusto). Rubber boots needed. Mosquitoes! Northern Finland mainly, but also southern bogs.
- **Lampone selvatico** (Raspberry, Rubus idaeus, FI: vadelma) — Jul-Aug. Clear-cut areas and roadsides. Check for worms before eating.
- **Mirtillo palustre** (Bog bilberry, Vaccinium uliginosum, FI: juolukka) — August. Elongated shape vs round bilberry. Taller shrub. Wet heaths, bog edges, lake shores. More vitamin C than bilberry. NOT toxic (urban legend).
- **Fragola selvatica** (Wild strawberry, Fragaria vesca, FI: metsämansikka) — Jun-Jul. Extremely sweet. Roadside edges, meadows, clearings. Finnish tradition: thread on hay strand.
- **Ossicocco** (Cranberry, Vaccinium oxycoccos, FI: karpalo) — Sep-Oct (until snow). Bogs and wetlands. Tart. Jams, juices. Rich vitamin C.
- **Moretta** (Crowberry, Empetrum nigrum, FI: variksenmarja) — Aug onwards. Black, glossy, white crispy interior. Dry heaths, bog hummocks. More antioxidants than bilberry. Bountiful harvest.
- **Olivello spinoso** (Sea buckthorn, Hippophae rhamnoides, FI: tyrni) — Coastal areas. Orange berries. Very rich in vitamin C.

### Mushrooms (200+ edible species, 5000 fungi total)
- **Gallinaccio/Finferlo** (Chanterelle, Cantharellus cibarius, FI: kantarelli) — Most popular. Jul-Oct. Yellow, funnel-shaped. Mossy conifer forests. Western Finnish tradition.
- **Porcino** (Porcini/Cep, Boletus edulis, FI: herkkutatti) — Aug-Oct. Brown cap, white pores. Pine and spruce forests. Highly prized.
- **Finferla invernale** (Winter chanterelle/Yellowfoot, Craterellus tubaeformis, FI: suppilovahvero) — Sep-Nov. Brown cap, yellow stem. Abundant in mossy spruce forests.
- **Lattario** (Milk caps, Lactarius spp., FI: rouskut) — Eastern Finnish tradition. Acrid when raw, salted/pickled. Multiple species.
- **Ovolo malefico** (Fly agaric, Amanita muscaria) — TOXIC. Red with white spots. Common but DO NOT eat.
- **Spugnola** (Morel, Morchella spp., FI: korvasieni) — Spring (Apr-May). Requires boiling before eating (contains toxins removed by heat).

### Wildflowers & Ground Plants
- **Mughetto** (Lily of the valley, Convallaria majalis, FI: kielo) — NATIONAL FLOWER. May-Jun. White bell-shaped flowers. Fragrant. TOXIC berries (red).
- **Geranio sanguigno** (Bloody crane's-bill, Geranium sanguineum) — Pink-purple flowers. Rocky areas.
- **Rosmarino di palude** (Marsh Labrador tea, Rhododendron tomentosum, FI: suopursu) — Fragrant white blooms in wetlands. Late spring/early summer. Aromatic leaves used traditionally.
- **Rosa di Lapponia** (Lapland rosebay, Rhododendron lapponicum, FI: lapinalppiruusu) — Rare, purple-pink. Arctic regions.
- **Linnea boreale** (Twinflower, Linnaea borealis, FI: vanamo) — Tiny pink paired flowers. Named after Linnaeus. Conifer forest floors.
- **Epilobio** (Fireweed/Rosebay willowherb, Epilobium angustifolium, FI: maitohorsma) — Pink spikes. Pioneer on burnt/cleared areas. Jul-Aug.
- **Ninfea bianca** (White water lily, Nymphaea alba, FI: valkolumme) — Lakes and ponds. Jun-Aug. Large white flowers.
- **Ninfea gialla** (Yellow water lily, Nuphar lutea, FI: ulpukka) — Lakes. Jun-Aug. Smaller yellow flowers.
- **Lichene delle renne** (Reindeer lichen, Cladonia rangiferina, FI: poronjäkälä) — Grey-green. Dry pine forests and tundra. Main winter food for reindeer. Grows 3-5mm/year.
- **Sfagno** (Sphagnum moss, FI: rahkasammal) — Peatlands. Holds 20x its weight in water. Builds peat over millennia.
- **Erica** (Heather, Calluna vulgaris, FI: kanerva) — Purple flowers Aug-Sep. Dry heaths and rocky areas.
- **Mirtillo di palude** (Bog rosemary, Andromeda polifolia, FI: suokukka) — Pink flowers. Bogs. TOXIC.

## FAUNA — DETAILED

### Mammals — Big 4 Carnivores
- **Orso bruno** (Brown bear, Ursus arctos, FI: karhu) — NATIONAL ANIMAL. ~2,000 individuals. 150-300 kg males. Eastern Finland (Kainuu, North Karelia). Omnivore (berries, ants, fish, carrion). Hibernates Oct-Apr. Cubs born Jan-Feb in den. Best viewing: June from hides. Ancient Finns had dozens of euphemisms to avoid saying "karhu" (sacred animal). Sightings from purpose-built hides in Kuhmo, Suomussalmi, Lieksa.
- **Lupo** (Wolf, Canis lupus, FI: susi) — ~300 individuals. Pack animals. Eastern Finland near Russian border. Elusive. Occasional bonus sighting from bear hides. Controversial (livestock conflicts).
- **Lince** (Eurasian lynx, Lynx lynx, FI: ilves) — ~2,000-2,500 individuals. Throughout Finnish forests. 18-30 kg. Ear tufts distinctive. Hunts hares, roe deer. Very secretive, rarely seen. Acute senses detect humans long before reverse.
- **Ghiottone** (Wolverine, Gulo gulo, FI: ahma) — ~250-300 individuals. Largest land-dwelling mustelid. 10-25 kg but incredibly strong. Lieksa (North Karelia) = best place in world to see them. Erä-Eero Wildlife Centre. Year-round sightings. Solitary, vast territories.

### Other Mammals
- **Alce** (Elk/Moose, Alces alces, FI: hirvi) — Up to 700 kg, 2m at shoulder. Found everywhere in Finland. Active dawn/dusk. Marshes, meadows, forest edges. DANGER: road collisions. ~100,000 individuals.
- **Renna** (Reindeer, Rangifer tarandus, FI: poro) — ~200,000 semi-domesticated in Lapland (above ~65°N). Adapted to -50°C. See UV light. Herded by Sámi and Finnish reindeer herders. Frequently on roads — slow down!
- **Foca degli anelli del Saimaa** (Saimaa ringed seal, Pusa hispida saimensis, FI: saimaannorppa) — ~530 individuals (2025). ONLY in Lake Saimaa. Isolated from Baltic ~8,000 years ago after Ice Age. Recognized as own species 2025. Endangered. 60-90 kg, 130-145 cm. Lives 20-30 years. Pups born Feb-Mar on ice. Summer: hauls out on rocks. Best viewing: boat trips late spring/summer. Conservation success: up from <200 in 1980s.
- **Scoiattolo volante** (Siberian flying squirrel, Pteromys volans, FI: liito-orava) — Nocturnal. Old-growth mixed forests with aspen. Glides up to 50m between trees. EU protected. ~140g.
- **Lepre variabile** (Mountain hare, Lepus timidus, FI: metsäjänis) — White in winter, brown in summer. Throughout forests.
- **Castoro europeo** (European beaver, Castor fiber, FI: euroopanmajava) — Reintroduced. Lakes and rivers. Dams, lodges.
- **Volpe rossa** (Red fox, Vulpes vulpes, FI: kettu) — Common throughout.
- **Volpe artica** (Arctic fox, Vulpes lagopus, FI: naali) — Critically endangered in Finland. Only in northernmost Lapland fells. <10 individuals.

### Birds
- **Cigno canoro** (Whooper swan, Cygnus cygnus, FI: laulujoutsen) — NATIONAL BIRD. Returns spring (Apr-May). Nests on lakeshores. Flocks of hundreds at stopover points. Wingspan 2.1-2.4m. Loud trumpeting call.
- **Aquila di mare** (White-tailed eagle, Haliaeetus albicilla, FI: merikotka) — Europe's largest eagle. Wingspan up to 2.5m. Baltic coast and archipelago. Conservation success story (back from brink). Boat trips in SW archipelago.
- **Gufo della Lapponia** (Great grey owl, Strix nebulosa, FI: lapinpöllö) — World's longest owl (up to 70cm). Northern/central forests. Concentric eye rings. Hunts voles in open areas.
- **Ghiandaia siberiana** (Siberian jay, Perisoreus infaustus, FI: kuukkeli) — Bold, sociable. Lapland forests. Approaches hikers. Inspects food from close range.
- **Falco pescatore** (Osprey, Pandion haliaetus, FI: sääksi) — Fish-hunting raptor. Dives feet-first into lakes. Nests on tall trees/poles near water. Common in Lakeland.
- **Strolaga mezzana** (Black-throated loon/diver, Gavia arctica, FI: kuikka) — Iconic lake bird. Haunting call. Dives for fish. Nests on lake islets.
- **Quattrocchi** (Common goldeneye, Bucephala clangula, FI: telkkä) — Finland = breeding site for majority of world's goldeneyes. Cavity nester (uses nest boxes). Lakes.
- **Gallo cedrone** (Capercaillie, Tetrao urogallus, FI: metso) — Largest grouse. Males up to 6 kg. Spectacular spring display (lek). Old-growth pine forests.
- **Fagiano di monte** (Black grouse, Lyrurus tetrix, FI: teeri) — Males glossy black with lyre-shaped tail. Spring lek on bogs.
- **Gru** (Common crane, Grus grus, FI: kurki) — Tall, grey. Bogs and wetlands. Trumpet call. Migrates south autumn.
- **Sterna artica** (Arctic tern, Sterna paradisaea, FI: lapintiira) — Longest migration of any animal (Arctic to Antarctic). Nests on coastal islands.
- **Mugnaiaccio** (Great black-backed gull, Larus marinus, FI: merilokki) — Largest gull. Coastal islands.

### Fish
- **Salmone** (Atlantic salmon, Salmo salar, FI: lohi) — Northern rivers (Teno/Tana, Tornio). Anadromous. Up to 20+ kg.
- **Trota** (Brown trout, Salmo trutta, FI: taimen) — Rivers and lakes. Endangered in many areas.
- **Coregone** (Whitefish, Coregonus lavaretus, FI: siika) — Much esteemed. Lakes and Baltic. Multiple forms.
- **Lucioperca** (Zander/Pike-perch, Sander lucioperca, FI: kuha) — Prized table fish. Warm, turbid lakes.
- **Luccio** (Pike, Esox lucius, FI: hauki) — Up to 15+ kg. Ambush predator. All Finnish waters.
- **Pesce persico** (European perch, Perca fluviatilis, FI: ahven) — Most common sport fish. Lakes and Baltic.
- **Salmerino** (Arctic char, Salvelinus alpinus, FI: nieriä) — Cold, deep lakes. Lapland.
- **Gambero di fiume** (Noble crayfish, Astacus astacus, FI: rapu/jokirapu) — Brief summer season (Jul-Aug). Crayfish parties (rapujuhlat) are tradition. Endangered by crayfish plague.
- **Aringa del Baltico** (Baltic herring, Clupea harengus membras, FI: silakka) — Most common sea fish. Smaller than Atlantic herring. Helsinki Market Square.

### Insects & Others
- **Zanzare** (Mosquitoes, FI: hyttyset) — Abundant Jun-Aug, especially near water and bogs. Peak in July. Repellent essential!
- **Libellule** (Dragonflies) — Many species over lakes.
- **Farfalle** (Butterflies) — Including Apollo butterfly (Parnassius apollo) in rocky areas.

## GEOLOGY — DETAILED

### Bedrock (Fennoscandian Shield)
- Among oldest exposed rock on Earth: 1.9-3.5 billion years
- **Granito** — National stone. Multiple types. Most common rock.
- **Gneiss** — Metamorphic. Archean age (>2.5 Ga) in east/north.
- **Granito rapakivi** — "Crumbly stone" in Finnish. 1.65-1.54 Ga. SE Finland + Åland. Large feldspar crystals with plagioclase rims that weather faster → crumbles. Distinctive reddish-brown.
- **Quarzite** — Metamorphosed sandstone. Koli National Park.
- **Ofioliti** — Jormua, Outokumpu, Nuttio complexes. Paleoproterozoic.
- **Carbonatite** — Siilinjärvi: one of Earth's oldest carbonatites (Archean).
- **Kimberlite** — Near Kuopio/Kaavi. Diamond-bearing pipes.

### Glacial History
- Quaternary glaciations: cyclic over last 2 million years
- Last glaciation (Weichselian): started ~118,000 years ago
- Maximum extent: ~25,000 years ago, ice 2 km thick over all Finland
- Ice sheet center: Gulf of Bothnia
- Eroded ~25m of rock on average (7m in some estimates for last glaciation alone)
- Material transported to Germany, Poland, Russia, Baltics
- First ice-free: SE coast, shortly before Younger Dryas (12,700 BP)
- Finland fully ice-free: ~9,000 BP

### Glacial Landforms
- **Esker** (harju): Ridges of sand/gravel from subglacial meltwater tunnels. Punkaharju = world-famous (7 km, 10m wide minimum, 31m above water). Important groundwater sources. Sandy soil, pine forests on top.
- **Drumlin**: Droplet-shaped till ridges. Direction shows ice flow. Few meters tall, up to km long. Pieksämäki area.
- **Salpausselkä I & II**: Two massive ice-marginal moraines. Formed 12,300-12,100 and 11,800-11,600 years ago during Younger Dryas cold spell. Visible from space. Run E-W across southern Finland. Boulder-rich proximal side, sandy delta distal side.
- **Moreena di fondo** (Ground moraine/till): Covers most bedrock. Few meters thick. Dense, unsorted.
- **Argilla varvitica** (Varved clay): Layered clay from glacial meltwater. Pale=summer, dark=winter. Like tree rings. Deep-water deposits.
- **Massi erratici** (Erratics): Boulders transported by ice far from origin.
- **Rocce montonate** (Rôche moutonnées): Polished rocky hills. Smooth upstream, rough downstream.
- **Striature glaciali** (Glacial striations): Scratches on bedrock from ice movement.

### Lake Formation
- 188,000 lakes (168,000 per some counts)
- Formed by: glacial erosion of weak bedrock zones, meltwater filling depressions between moraines/eskers, damming by moraines
- Lake Saimaa: 4th largest in Europe (4,400 km²), 14,000+ islands, max depth 82m. Labyrinthine shape from fractured bedrock + glacial features. Part of Vuoksi watershed → drains to Lake Ladoga → Baltic.
- Saimaa UNESCO Global Geopark

### Post-glacial Rebound (Isostatic)
- Land still rising from ice sheet weight removal
- Rate: 5-9 mm/year on Ostrobothnia coast (fastest in Finland)
- ~3 mm/year in Lakeland
- New land emerging from sea continuously
- Has raised former seabed above water → flat coastal plains

## NATURAL PHENOMENA — DETAILED

### Midnight Sun / White Nights
- **Arctic Circle (66.5°N)**: Sun doesn't set Jun 6 - Jul 7 (31 days)
- **Utsjoki (70°N, northernmost)**: Sun doesn't set May 17 - Jul 27 (73 days!)
- **Sodankylä (67.4°N)**: ~40 days of midnight sun
- **Lakeland / Punkaharju (61.8°N)**: NOT true midnight sun, but:
  - Jun 21 (solstice): sun sets ~23:30, rises ~03:00 → only ~3.5h below horizon
  - Never gets truly dark (civil twilight all night)
  - ~20h of direct sunlight
  - "White nights" (valoisat yöt): sky remains bright, reddish-golden light
- **Helsinki (60.2°N)**: ~19h of daylight at solstice. Sun barely dips below horizon.
- Light quality: reddish-yellow, like perpetual golden hour/sunset
- Biological effects: reduced sleep, frantic plant growth, 24h bird singing, berries ripen fast
- Cultural: Juhannus (Midsummer) celebration, bonfires, no bedtime for children

### Noctilucent Clouds (NLC)
- Highest clouds in atmosphere: ~80-85 km altitude (mesosphere)
- Visible Jun-Jul in Finland (latitude 55-70°N optimal)
- Appear after sunset when sun is 6-16° below horizon
- Electric blue/silver glow
- Made of ice crystals on meteor dust
- Best viewing: looking north, 1-2h after sunset
- More common in recent decades (possibly climate change related)

### Aurora Borealis
- NOT visible in summer (too much light)
- In Lapland: visible ~200 nights/year (Sep-Mar)
- In southern Finland: ~20 nights/year
- Caused by solar wind particles hitting atmosphere
- Colors: green (oxygen 100km), red (oxygen 200km), purple/blue (nitrogen)

### Other Phenomena
- **Ruska** (autumn colors): Sep-Oct. Birch yellow, bilberry/bog bilberry red, aspen orange. Starts in Lapland, moves south.
- **Kaamos** (polar night): Winter counterpart. NOT relevant for summer trip.
- **Halot** (Halos/sun dogs): Ice crystal optical phenomena. More common in winter but possible in cold mornings.

## ECOSYSTEMS — DETAILED

### Boreal Forest (Taiga)
- 75% of Finland (22.8 million ha)
- Biome characteristics: long cold winters (-20 to -40°C), short warm summers (15-25°C), 500-700mm precipitation
- Layers: canopy (pine/spruce 20-30m) → understory (birch, rowan, juniper) → shrub layer (bilberry, lingonberry, heather) → ground layer (mosses, lichens) → soil (thin, acidic, podzol)
- Fire ecology: historically fire every 46-122 years maintained diversity. Now virtually eliminated → spruce dominance increasing
- Biodiversity: 31 trees, 350 vascular plants, 5000 fungi, 11000 invertebrates, 76 birds, 36 mammals
- Carbon storage: significant but less than peatlands per hectare
- Threats: commercial forestry (monocultures), climate change (northward shift)

### Peatlands (Suo)
- ~10 million hectares (30% of Finland's land area!)
- Types: raised bogs (ombrotrophic), fens (minerotrophic), mires, aapa mires (Lapland)
- Formation: waterlogged conditions → incomplete decomposition → peat accumulates 0.5-1mm/year
- Depth: typically 1-5m, max >10m
- Carbon storage: enormous (5.7 billion tonnes CO2 equivalent in Finnish peatlands)
- Flora: Sphagnum mosses (build the peat), cloudberry, cranberry, sundew (carnivorous!), bog rosemary, cotton grass
- Fauna: cranes, curlews, golden plovers, short-eared owls, cloudberry-eating bears
- ~50% of Finnish peatlands drained for forestry/agriculture (now restoration efforts)
- Mosquito breeding grounds!

### Lake Ecosystem (Järvi-Suomi)
- 188,000 lakes (10% of land area is water)
- Characteristics: oligotrophic (low nutrients), clear water, visibility 2-5m, slightly acidic (pH 6-7)
- Thermal stratification: summer surface 18-22°C, deep water 4-8°C
- Food web: phytoplankton → zooplankton → small fish (vendace, smelt) → predators (pike, zander, perch) → osprey, white-tailed eagle, Saimaa seal
- Aquatic plants: water lilies (white Nymphaea, yellow Nuphar), pondweeds (Potamogeton), reeds (Phragmites), bulrush (Typha)
- Saimaa specifically: 4,400 km², 14,000+ islands, max depth 82m, labyrinthine channels, home to endemic seal
- Ice cover: typically Dec-Apr/May. Important for seal pupping.
- Threats: eutrophication (agriculture runoff), climate change (shorter ice season threatens seals)

### Archipelago/Coastal (Baltic)
- Brackish water (salinity 5-7‰ vs ocean 35‰)
- Unique mix: freshwater species (pike, perch) + marine species (herring, flounder)
- Thousands of islands (SW archipelago = largest in world by island count)
- White-tailed eagle territory
- Grey seal, ringed seal (Baltic subspecies)
- Seabird colonies: arctic tern, black-backed gulls, eider duck

### Fell/Tundra (Lapland)
- Above treeline (~600-700m in north, lower further north)
- Dwarf birch, willow, lichens, mosses
- Ptarmigan, snowy owl, Arctic fox (critically endangered)
- Reindeer grazing lands
- Permafrost: sporadic in northernmost Finland (palsa mires)


## ADDITIONAL FINDINGS

### Lady's Slipper Orchid (Cypripedium calceolus)
- Finnish: tikankontti
- One of Finland's largest flowers (up to 10 cm diameter)
- Near threatened, PROTECTED in all of Finland
- Perennial herb, 20-50 cm tall
- Flowers: reddish-brown tepals + yellow pouch-like labellum
- Blooms: June
- Habitat: broad-leaved forests, fens, rich mixed bogs, calcicolous (lime-loving)
- Pollination: complex trap mechanism — small bees fall into labellum, can only exit through corridor past pollen
- Fragrance mimics burrowing bees' pheromones
- Source: NatureGate/LuontoPortti

### Carnivorous Plants in Finnish Bogs
- **Drosera rotundifolia** (Round-leaved sundew, FI: pyöreälehtikihokki) — Most common. Found wherever Sphagnum grows. Sticky tentacles trap insects. Up to 5 insects/month.
- **Drosera anglica** (Great sundew, FI: pitkälehtikihokki) — Longer leaves, rarer
- **Pinguicula vulgaris** (Common butterwort, FI: yhtäläinen tulivuori) — Pale violet flowers. Sticky leaves trap insects. Fens and wet rocks.
- All compensate for nutrient-poor bog conditions by digesting insects for nitrogen/phosphorus

### Midnight Sun — Precise Data
- White nights (no true darkness): latitude 60°34'N and above in June
- True midnight sun: latitude 65°44'N and above in June
- Finland's northernmost point (Utsjoki, 70°N): sun doesn't set for 73 days (May 17 - Jul 27)
- Punkaharju/Savonlinna area (~61.8°N): White nights. Sun barely dips below horizon. ~20h direct sunlight at solstice. Never truly dark Jun-Jul.
- Helsinki (60.2°N): ~19h daylight at solstice. Brief twilight, not full darkness.
- The table from Wikipedia confirms: lowest latitude for white nights in June = 59°34'N (all of Finland qualifies!)

### Saimaa Geopark — Key Details
- Bedrock: 1.9-1.6 billion years old (Paleoproterozoic)
- Rapakivi granite: characteristic of SE Finland. "Rapa" = mud/sand (rapautua = to erode), "kivi" = rock. Crumbles due to different thermal expansion of minerals. Large rounded orthoclase crystals with oligoclase rims. First described by Jakob Sederholm 1891. Wiborg batholith: 1646-1627 Ma. Used as "Baltic Brown" building stone.
- Salpausselkä: formed during Younger Dryas cold period (12,800 years ago). I: 12,300-12,100 BP. II: 11,800-11,600 BP. Boulder-rich proximal side, sandy delta distal side.
- Eskers: sand/gravel deposited in meltwater tunnels inside glacier. Important groundwater sources.
- Drumlins: droplet-shaped, few meters tall, up to km long. Fan-like fields. Rocky core at head.
- Varved clay: pale=summer (more melting), dark=winter (less melting). Like tree rings.
- Ground moraine: dense, unsorted, covers most bedrock, few meters thick.
- Imatra stones: chalk/limestone concretions formed in clay deposits. Unique to Vuoksi area.

### Land Uplift (Isostatic Rebound)
- Known since 1491 (Östhammar harbour complaint)
- Fastest: Quark/Kvarken area (Gulf of Bothnia) = nearly 10 mm/year
- Slowest in Finland: SE corner = less than 3 mm/year
- Still ~100m to rise near Gulf of Bothnia
- Ice was 2 km thick, depressed crust by ~500m
- Current global sea level rise (~1.5 mm/yr, accelerating) partially offsets uplift
- In Finland, uplift still exceeds sea level rise → new land still appearing
- Monitored by precise levelling (3 campaigns: 1892-1910, 1935-1975, 1978-2006) and GNSS


## FISH — DETAILED (from fishinginfinland.fi)
- **Pesce persico** (Perch, Perca fluviatilis, FI: ahven) — NATIONAL FISH. Most common game species. Found throughout except northernmost. Strong stocks. 0.5-1 kg common.
- **Luccio** (Pike, Esox lucius, FI: hauki) — 2nd most common. Found throughout in almost all waters. Metre-long fish possible. Baltic coast + entire lake area.
- **Lucioperca** (Zander, Sander lucioperca, FI: kuha) — Best stocks in Lake District of Southern/Central Finland. Dark/muddy waters preferred. Northernmost zander lake = Kemijärvi. Warming climate strengthening stocks.
- **Coregone** (Whitefish, Coregonus lavaretus, FI: siika) — Common throughout. Rivers, lakes, Baltic coast. Shy, pursued with small flies. Considered best food fish. Lively fighter.
- **Temolo** (Grayling, Thymallus thymallus, FI: harjus) — Beautiful fish of pure northern waters. Best in Lapland. Rivers Tornionjoki, Teno, Lake Inarijärvi.
- **Salmone** (Atlantic salmon, Salmo salar, FI: lohi) — Wild in Tenojoki, Näätämöjoki (Arctic Ocean), Tornionjoki, Simojoki (Gulf of Bothnia). Tornionjoki = Europe's largest natural salmon river. Tenojoki fishing banned due to declining stocks.
- **Trota** (Brown trout, Salmo trutta, FI: taimen) — Wild mostly in Lapland. 30 original stocks survive, majority endangered. Restocking programs.
- **Salmerino** (Arctic char, Salvelinus alpinus, FI: nieriä) — Pristine waters of northernmost Lapland. Lake Inarijärvi, Kilpisjärvi. Cool waters. Red-bellied males during spawning = most beautiful fish.
- **Abramide** (Bream, Abramis brama, FI: lahna) — Common in Southern/Central Finland lakes. Bottom feeder. Mid-June to end August best.

### Crayfish (from various sources)
- **Noble crayfish** (Astacus astacus, FI: jokirapu/rapu) — Season: July 21 - October
- **Rapujuhlat** (crayfish party) — Traditional Finnish/Swedish celebration in August
- Crayfish boiled with dill, eaten communally, paper hats, lanterns, schnapps
- Native noble crayfish endangered by crayfish plague (Aphanomyces astaci) brought by American signal crayfish
- Signal crayfish (Pacifastacus leniusculus) now more common, introduced 1960s

## SAIMAA RINGED SEAL — DETAILED
- Scientific name: Pusa hispida saimensis (recognized as own species 2025, previously subspecies)
- Population: ~530 (2025), up from <200 in 1980s
- ONLY habitat: Lake Saimaa, Finland
- Size: 60-90 kg, 130-145 cm
- Lifespan: 20-30 years
- Isolation: separated from Baltic Sea ~8,000 years ago by post-glacial land uplift
- Breeding: pups born late February/early March in lairs (pesä) dug into snowdrifts on lake ice
- Lairs protect pups from predators and cold (-20°C outside, ~0°C inside lair)
- Pups suckled for ~2 months
- Summer behavior: hauls out on rocks to bask, especially late spring/early summer
- Diet: mainly perch, vendace, smelt, ruffe
- Threats: climate change (less ice/snow for lairs), fishing nets (bycatch of pups), disturbance
- Conservation: fishing restrictions (gillnets banned in breeding areas Apr 15 - Jun 30), artificial snowdrifts built by volunteers, speed limits on boats
- Viewing: boat trips from Savonlinna/Puumala area, late May-July. Keep 50m+ distance.
- Listed as Endangered (IUCN)
- Source: Visit Finland, WWF Arctic, Metsähallitus, NOAA
