# Nature in Finland

Finland offers one of the purest and wildest ecosystems in Europe. This nature section explores the biological and geological richness of the country, with a particular focus on the Lakeland region (Saimaa) crossed by the itinerary.

## 🌲 Flora: The boreal forest and the undergrowth
Finland is the most forested country in Europe, with over 70% of its land covered by the **taiga** (boreal forest). The landscape is dominated by evergreen conifers such as Scots pine (*Pinus sylvestris*) and Norway spruce (*Picea abies*), accompanied by silver birch, easily recognised by its white bark, and rowan.

The true treasure of Finnish flora is hidden in the undergrowth, which in summer fills with wild berries and mushrooms, freely gatherable thanks to "Everyman's Right" (Jokamiehenoikeus). Between July and August the **bilberry** (smaller and more flavourful than the cultivated variety) and the **lingonberry** ripen. In the boggy areas grows the rare and prized **cloudberry**, known as "the gold of the bogs" — an amber berry rich in vitamin C. The most sought-after mushrooms are chanterelles and porcini, which appear in abundance after the summer rains. Along lake shores white and yellow water-lilies bloom, while the bogs are scented with bog-rosemary (*Rhododendron tomentosum*).

## 🦌 Wildlife: Lords of the Taiga
The Finnish forests are home to Europe’s "big four" carnivores: the **brown bear** (Finland’s national animal, with around 2,000 individuals), the wolf, the lynx and the wolverine. Although elusive, the eastern areas near the Russian border offer excellent chances of sightings from purpose-built hides.

More likely to be encountered along the roads are the majestic **moose** (which can weigh up to 700 kg) and, heading north into Lapland, semi-domesticated **reindeer**. But the true emblem of the lake region is the very rare **Saimaa ringed seal** (*Pusa hispida saimensis*). Isolated from the Baltic Sea after the last Ice Age around 8,000 years ago, this freshwater seal is one of the world’s most endangered, with only ~530 individuals remaining. The summer skies are traversed by the **whooper swan** (the national bird whose return in spring marks the end of winter), the osprey and the majestic white-tailed sea eagle.

## 🪨 Geology: The Legacy of the Ice Age
The Finnish landscape is an open book on the last Ice Age. The bedrock is part of the **Fennoscandian Shield**, one of the oldest geological formations on Earth (1.9–1.6 billion years), made mainly of granite (the national rock) and gneiss. In the south-eastern area there is the distinctive *rapakivi* granite, known for its tendency to crumble.

Around 25,000 years ago an immense ice sheet, two kilometres thick, covered the whole of Finland. As it retreated, the ice sculpted the landscape and left behind spectacular features:
- **Esker**: long, sinuous ridges of sand and gravel deposited by subglacial rivers. The *Punkaharju Ridge* is one of the most famous eskers in the world.
- **Moraines and drumlins**: teardrop-shaped hills modelled by the movement of ice.
- The **Salpausselkä**: two massive marginal moraine systems visible even from space, formed when the glaciers’ retreat temporarily halted.
The melting ice filled the depressions in the land, creating the **188,000 lakes** that today define Finland, including Saimaa, the fourth largest lake in Europe.

## 🌌 Natural phenomena: The Midnight Sun
The Finnish summer is defined by light. Because of the tilt of the Earth's axis, during the summer months the sun never sets above the Arctic Circle, creating the phenomenon of the **Midnight Sun** (or a "night without night").

Even in the lake district (around the 61st parallel), where there isn’t a true midnight sun, you experience **white nights**. The sun dips just below the horizon for a few hours, but it never gets fully dark: the light takes on a reddish-golden hue, like a perpetual sunset. This disrupts biological rhythms (people sleep fewer hours) and nature erupts into frantic growth. In summer, because of the excess light, you can’t see the Northern Lights, but you can sometimes spot the rare **noctilucent clouds**, the highest clouds in the Earth’s atmosphere (about 80 km up) that glow with a bluish-silver light after sunset.

## 🌳 Ecosystems: A mosaic of water and forest
The Finnish environment is an intricate mosaic where water and land merge. The three main ecosystems are:
- **The Boreal Forest (Taiga)**: a resilient biome adapted to harsh winters and short summers, acting as a vast green lung and habitat for countless species.
- **Peatlands (Suo)**: Finland has about 10 million hectares of peatlands, bogs and marshes (around 30% of the land). These environments, often carpeted with sphagnum moss, are crucial carbon stores and the exclusive habitat of the cloudberry and cranes.
- **The Lake Ecosystem (Järvi-Suomi)**: the waters of Finland’s lakes are oligotrophic (low in nutrients but exceptionally clear and clean). This freshwater ecosystem hosts prized fish such as pike, perch, whitefish (siika) and zander (kuha), and provides refuge to the Saimaa ringed seal in a labyrinth of more than 14,000 islands.
