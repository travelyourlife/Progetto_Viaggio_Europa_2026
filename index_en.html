🛡️ Plan B — Ferragosto Bypass via Zaragoza + Camargue (RECOMMENDED)

You sacrifice the Costa Brava but completely avoid Ferragosto traffic. The Camargue is an excellent alternative with children (horses, flamingos, free beaches).

D Date Route Km Hours Strategic notes
D49 Thu 13/08 Palencia ➔ Zaragoza 350 4h Calm trip post-eclipse. Afternoon tapas + Basilica del Pilar
D50 Fri 14/08 Zaragoza ➔ Camargue 🇫🇷 580 6h 30m Cross the border BEFORE the weekend. Arrival Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer evening

Pros: Avoid black alert. Better distributed kilometers. Camargue = pure nature, perfect for children. Zaragoza is worth it (Pilar, tapas, river Aquarium).

Cons: You lose Cadaqués/Cap de Creus. D50 (580 km) is still demanding — but on Friday morning the A9 is still flowing.

📍 Camargue — what to do with children:

➕ Horse ride on the beach (many stables accept children 6+)

🧑‍🎨 Figures to Know

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756-1791) — Composed his first symphony at 8 years old (the same age as your eldest child!). At 5 he was already playing for empresses. In Vienna he wrote The Marriage of Figaro, Don Giovanni, and The Magic Flute. He died at 35, buried in a common grave — no one knows exactly where.

📍 Mozarthaus Vienna — Mozart's only surviving Viennese apartment

📍 mozarthausvienna.at

Empress Sisi (Elisabeth of Bavaria, 1837-1898) — The rebellious empress who hated court life, traveled constantly, exercised 3 hours a day, and wrote poetry. Children love her because she was "the princess who didn't want to be a princess".

📍 Sisi Museum, Hofburg

Gustav Klimt (1862-1918) — The painter of The Kiss (all gold!). Children recognize him immediately for the bright colors and geometric patterns. He used real gold in his works! His studio was full of cats (he had 7).

📍 Belvedere Museum — here the original "The Kiss" is exhibited

📍 belvedere.at

Ludwig van Beethoven (1770-1827) — German by birth, but lived in Vienna for 35 years. He became completely deaf at 44 but kept composing! The Ninth Symphony (with the "Ode to Joy", now EU anthem) was written when he could no longer hear anything. At the first performance, the audience applauded standing but he didn't notice — they had to turn him towards the audience.

📍 Beethoven Museum — the house where he composed the Ninth

🏛 What to observe in the architecture

🏛️ Imperial Baroque: yellow-gold facades (the "Schönbrunn yellow"), statues on the roofs, twisted columns. Look up! 📍 Maps

🏛️ Jugendstil (Art Nouveau): the Vienna Secession — buildings with golden floral decorations, especially along the Linke Wienzeile. The Secession building has a dome of 3,000 golden laurel leaves.

🏛️ St. Stephen's Cathedral (Stephansdom): the mosaic roof with 230,000 colored tiles forms the Habsburg eagle. Kids can count the colors! 📍 Maps

🏛️ Hundertwasserhaus: an apartment building where no floor is straight, trees grow from the windows, and every facade is a different color. Hundertwasser hated straight lines — "man's enemy". Kids go crazy! 📍 Maps

📍 Hundertwasserhaus

🏛️ Schönbrunn: the imperial palace with 1,441 rooms (only 40 are visited!). The garden has a maze where kids can get lost and a zoo — the oldest in the world (1752). 📍 Maps

📍 Schönbrunn Palace

🎉 Living traditions in summer (July)

🏛️ Wiener Festwochen (Vienna festival) — free outdoor shows until mid-June

🏛️ Donauinselfest — the largest open-air music festival in Europe (end of June, free!)

💡 Viennese people in summer dine at Heurigen (taverns with gardens) drinking local wine and eating cold platters

🏛️ Film Festival Rathausplatz (July-August) — FREE open-air cinema in front of the town hall with classical music concerts on a giant screen + international food stalls. Perfect for the D1 evening! 📍 Maps

📍 Rathausplatz

💡 Viennese children in summer go to the Donauinsel (island on the Danube) — 42 km of free river beaches, bike paths, and playgrounds

🏛️ Fiaker (horse carriages) — expensive (~€80/20min) but kids love them. Alternative: the tourist tram Ring Tram (€12 adult, free under 6 years)

  • Golden eagle — Wingspan up to 2.2m. Symbol of the Alps. 📚

🎵 Music & Art

  • Mozart — Born in Salzburg in 1756. Composed his first piece at age 5! 📚
  • Strauss waltz — "The Blue Danube" is practically Austria's second anthem. 📚
  • Gustav Klimt — "The Kiss" (1907) is Austria's most famous painting. Gold and Art Nouveau. 📚

🎯 Quiz for kids

  1. "At what age did Mozart write his first symphony?" → 8! (like you!)

  2. "How old is the Prater wheel?" → More than 125! (1897)

  3. "What did the Turks leave when they fled Vienna?" → Coffee! That's why Viennese people are obsessed with coffee.

  4. "How many tiles does the Cathedral roof have?" → 230,000! They form a giant eagle.

  5. "Was Beethoven deaf when he wrote the Ninth Symphony?" → Yes! Completely deaf. He didn't even hear the applause at the premiere.

  6. "How many rooms does Schönbrunn Palace have?" → 1,441! But you can only visit 40. 📍 Maps

  7. "Why is Hundertwasser's house crooked?" → Because he hated straight lines! He said they were "man's enemy".

  8. "What animal is the symbol of Austria?" → The eagle! It's on their coat of arms holding a hammer and a sickle.

  9. "How tall is the tower of St. Stephen's Cathedral?" → 136 meters! It took 65 years to build.

  10. "What famous cake was invented in Vienna?" → The Sachertorte! A chocolate cake with apricot jam inside.

  11. "Mozart had a very long name. How many names did he have?" → Seven! Johannes Chrysostomus Wolfgangus Theophilus Mozart. But everyone called him Wolfgang.

  12. "Why do Austrian cows wear bells?" → To find them in the mountain fog! And they have a festival when the cows come back to the valley in autumn.

  13. "How do you say 'good morning' in Austrian?" → Grüß Gott! 🙏 It literally means "God greet you" — everyone says it, even children!

  14. "How many children did Empress Sissi have?" → Four! But she was so sad that she traveled constantly and hardly ever stayed at the palace.

  15. "What shape is the Kaiserschmarrn dessert?" → It's a sweet torn pancake! 🥞 The name means "Emperor's mess" because it was his favorite.

  16. "Why is Vienna called Wien in German?" → From the Wien river that flows through the city! The Romans called it Vindobona.

  17. "How many violinists play in the Vienna New Year's Concert?" → About 100! 🎻 And the whole world watches it on TV on January 1st.

  18. "What animal can you see at Schönbrunn Zoo?" → Giant pandas! 🐼 It's the oldest zoo in the world (1752) and has two pandas from China. 📍 Maps

  19. What is the name of the famous Viennese cake with apricot jam? Sachertorte! 🎂 It was invented in 1832 by Franz Sacher when he was only 16 years old.

  20. Which animal is the symbol of the Vienna Zoo (Schönbrunn)? The giant panda! 🐼 Schönbrunn is the oldest zoo in the world (1752). 📍 Maps

  21. What color is the famous Schönbrunn Palace? Yellow! 🟡 It's called 'Schönbrunn yellow' and was chosen by Empress Maria Theresa. 📍 Maps

  22. What is a 'Kaiserschmarrn'? A sweet torn pancake! 🥞 The name means 'Emperor's mess' because it was Emperor Franz Joseph's favorite.

🇦🇹 Austrian Cuisine (D1-D2)

📅 Itinerary D1-D2 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Austrian cuisine

Austrian cuisine is a crossroads of empires: 📚 Wikipedia. The Wiener Schnitzel likely came from Milan (cotoletta alla milanese), brought to Vienna in 1857 by Field Marshal Radetzky. Kaiserschmarrn ("Emperor's mess") was invented for Franz Joseph I when his cook botched a crêpe. Kaffeehäuser (Viennese coffeehouses, 📚 Wikipedia) were born after the Turkish siege of 1683: coffee sacks left by the Turks became a Viennese obsession. Today they're UNESCO heritage. The Sachertorte was created in 1832 by 16-year-old Franz Sacher for Prince Metternich — the original recipe is still secret!

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Tafelspitz — Boiled beef with chive sauce and grated apple. Emperor Franz Joseph's favourite dish. 📚
  • Knödel — Bread dumplings (Semmelknödel) or potato dumplings, served with roast or as sweet apricot-filled dessert (Marillenknödel). 📚
  • Palatschinken — Austrian crêpes filled with apricot jam or Nutella. Perfect for kids!
  • Leberkäse — "Liver cheese" (contains neither liver nor cheese!). Hot meatloaf served in a roll. Classic street food. 📚

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Würstel from Würstelstand — stalls open late (€3-5). Try the Käsekrainer (sausage filled with melted cheese!)
  • Naschmarkt (Vienna) — 120 stalls with food from all over the world. Perfect for lunch (€5-8/person). 📍 Maps
  • Bitzinger Würstelstand — the most famous stall, in front of the Opera (open until 4 am!). 📍 Maps

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Hofer (= Austrian Aldi) — lowest prices. Alpine cheeses (Bergkäse), Tyrolean speck, rye bread, excellent fresh milk etc.
  • Sachertorte — the most famous chocolate cake in the world. The supermarket one is good too (~€4)
  • Heurigen (wine taverns) — in Grinzing or Nussdorf. Cold platters (€15-20 for 4) with local bulk wine

🍳 Van recipes

  • Kaiserschmarrn express — 4 eggs, 200g flour, 200ml milk, butter, powdered sugar. Make a thick omelette, tear it with two forks, sprinkle with sugar. 10 minutes, loved by all!
  • Würstel with Kartoffelsalat — Frankfurter sausages (from Hofer fridge) heated in hot water + potato salad (boil potatoes, season with vinegar/oil/onion/mustard). Classic German dinner in 20 min.
  • Frittatensuppe — hot broth with strips of omelette (make a thin crêpe, roll it and cut into strips, dip in broth). Perfect for cool mountain evenings.

🍽️ To try at the local

  • Wiener Schnitzel — breaded veal cutlet fried in butter. The original is veal, not pork! It should "ripple" when you move it on the plate
  • Kaiserschmarrn — sweet omelette in pieces with powdered sugar and plum compote. Legend: invented for Emperor Franz Joseph!
  • Apfelstrudel — apple strudel with very thin filo pastry. With cream or vanilla ice cream
  • Germknödel — sweet steamed dumpling filled with plum jam, with vanilla sauce and poppy seeds

💡 Tips

  • Würstelstand are open late — perfect for a quick dinner after a late arrival
  • Hofer is the cheapest supermarket (= Aldi). Spar is good but more expensive
  • Tap water in Vienna is alpine spring water — among the best in Europe

🇨🇿 Czech Cuisine (D2-D3, transit)

📅 Itinerary D2-D3 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Czech cuisine

Czech cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) is hearty and filling, born to survive long winters. Czech beer has a 1,000-year tradition: Pilsner was invented in Plzeň in 1842 (📚 Wikipedia) — Czechs drink more beer per capita than anyone (140 litres/year!). The Trdelník (chimney cake) seen everywhere in Prague is actually Hungarian/Slovak, but has become the symbol of Prague tourism. Real Czech dishes are knedlíky (boiled bread dumplings) that accompany every meat dish.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Svíčková na smetaně — Beef sirloin with creamy vegetable sauce, cranberries and knedlíky. THE Czech national dish. 📚
  • Vepřo-knedlo-zelo — "Pork-dumplings-sauerkraut": the holy trinity of Czech cuisine. Roast pork, knedlíky and stewed sauerkraut.
  • Kulajda — Creamy porcini mushroom soup with potatoes, dill and poached egg. Perfect comfort food.
  • Koláče — Round pastries filled with poppy seeds, sweet cheese or plum jam. Every grandmother has her own recipe!

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Trdelník — warm chimney cake, sold everywhere in historic centers (~€3). Crunchy and sugary, kids love it
  • Bramboráky — potato pancakes (perfect street food for kids, ~€2-3)

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Lidl/Albert — excellent beer (also non-alcoholic), fresh bread, Hermelin cheeses (Czech Camembert type)
  • Kofola — Czech cola, less sweet than Coca-Cola, unique herbal taste (~€1)

🍳 Van recipes

  • Bramboráky express — grated potatoes + egg + flour + garlic + marjoram + salt. Fry as pancakes in a pan. 15 min, loved by kids!
  • Svíčková express — sliced chicken breast + cream + grated carrot + a spoon of mustard + bay leaf. Simmer 20 min on low heat. Serve with knedlíky (bread dumplings, ready-made in supermarkets) or bread. Perfect Czech comfort food for cool evenings!

🍽️ To try at the local

  • Svíčková — beef in creamy sauce with knedlíky (bread dumplings) and cranberries
  • Vepřo-knedlo-zelo — roast pork with knedlíky and sauerkraut (the national dish)

💡 Tips

  • Only a quick transit (D2-D3) — focus on Trdelník and beer!
  • Czech prices are about half of Italian ones

🇵🇱 Polish Cuisine (D3-D4)

📅 Itinerary D3-D4 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Polish cuisine

Polish cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) blends Slavic, Jewish and noble traditions. Pierogi (📚 Wikipedia) are dumplings brought from China via the Silk Road in the 13th century — today they're the national dish. Polish vodka has a 500+ year history (the word "vodka" first appears in a Polish document from 1405!). Bigos ("hunter's stew") improves every time it's reheated — noble families prepared it days before feasts. The Wigilia tradition (Christmas Eve dinner) requires 12 dishes, all meatless.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Bigos — "Hunter's stew": sauerkraut, mixed meats, sausages, dried mushrooms, prunes. Cooked for hours. 📚
  • Żurek — Sour soup of fermented rye flour with sausage and hard-boiled egg. Served inside a bread bowl! 📚
  • Oscypek — Smoked sheep cheese from the Tatra mountains. Spindle-shaped with carved decorations. Grilled and eaten with blueberry jam! 📚
  • Pączki — Polish doughnuts filled with rose jam. On Fat Thursday (Tłusty Czwartek) millions are eaten!

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Oscypek — smoked grilled cheese with blueberry jam. Sold at stalls everywhere (~PLN 10 (~€2.4))
  • Zapiekanka — open baguette with mushrooms, cheese and ketchup. The quintessential Polish street food (~PLN 12-15 (~€2.8–€3.5))
  • Pączki — doughnuts filled with rose jam or cream (the best in Europe!)
  • Gofry — giant waffles with toppings (Nutella, cream, fruit) sold everywhere in summer
  • Bar Mleczny Bambino (Warsaw) — popular canteen from the communist era still active. Pierogi + soup + compote = ~PLN 25 (~€5.9)/person! 📍 Maps
  • Hala Koszyki (Warsaw) — modern food hall with Polish and international cuisine. 📍 Maps

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Biedronka/Lidl — everything costs half of Italy. Stock up for Scandinavia! Buy: pasta, rice, preserves, snacks, Wedel chocolate, juices
  • Frozen Pierogi — supermarket ones are great too (Biedronka has an excellent fresh line, ~PLN 8 (~€1.9))
  • Żubrówka — bison grass vodka (souvenir for adults, ~PLN 30 (~€7))

🍳 Van recipes

  • Pierogi leniwe (lazy pierogi) — 500g ricotta, 1 egg, flour as needed for soft dough. Cut into gnocchi shapes, boil for 3 min, dress with melted butter and breadcrumbs. 15 minutes, loved by children!
  • Placki ziemniaczane — grated potatoes + egg + flour + grated onion + salt. Fry like golden pancakes. Serve with sour cream. 15 min, similar to Czech Bramboráky but thinner.
  • Polish tomato soup — tomato puree + broth + cream + short pasta (or rice). Polish children eat it every week — simple and delicious. 15 min.

🍽️ To try at the local place

  • Pierogi — stuffed ravioli (potato/cheese = "ruskie", meat = "z mięsem", fruit = "z owocami"). In "bar mleczny" cost ~PLN 15-20 (~€3.5–€4.7) for a huge portion!
  • Żurek — sour rye soup with sausage and egg, served in a bread bowl (you also eat the "plate"!)

💡 Tips

  • Stock up for Scandinavia! Polish prices are 50-60% lower than Scandinavian ones. Buy pasta, rice, preserves, snacks
  • Bar Mleczny (dairies) are popular canteens with homemade food at very low prices — look for them everywhere
  • Wedel chocolate is the best in Eastern Europe — buy bars as travel snacks

🇱🇹🇱🇻🇪🇪 Baltic Cuisine (D4-D7)

📅 Itinerary D4-D7 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Baltic cuisine

The cuisines of Lithuania, Latvia and Estonia (📚 Lithuanian, 📚 Latvian, 📚 Estonian) share common roots: dark rye bread, potatoes, herring, fermented dairy. Dark bread (Rugpjūtis/Rupjmaize) is sacred — throwing it away brings bad luck! Lithuania's farmhouse beer tradition is ancient: unfiltered, unpasteurised beers with recipes passed down for centuries. Estonia has strong Scandinavian influence (herring, salmon), Lithuania more Polish influence (pierogi/cepelinai), Latvia is in between.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Cepelinai (Lithuania) — Giant potato dumplings stuffed with meat, shaped like zeppelins! With sour cream and bacon. 📚
  • Šaltibarščiai (Lithuania) — Cold pink beetroot soup with kefir. Refreshing in summer! 📚
  • Piragi (Latvia) — Crescent-shaped rolls stuffed with bacon and onion. Christmas tradition but available year-round.
  • Verivorst (Estonia) — Blood sausage with barley and spices. Traditional winter dish.
  • Sklandrausis (Latvia) — Rye tart with carrot and potato filling. EU gastronomic heritage.

🌭 Street food / kiosks / take-away

  • Lido (Latvian chain) — self-service with local buffet cuisine. Huge variety, very low prices (~€5-7/person). Locations all over Riga
  • Riga Central Market (Centraltirgus) — in 5 pavilions (former Zeppelin hangars!) smoked fish, black bread, cheeses. Lunch for 4: ~€15-20. 📍 Maps
  • Kibinai — stuffed pastries (specialty of Trakai, Lithuania, ~€2-3 each)

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Maxima/Rimi/Selver — prices similar to Poland. Excellent dairy (sour cream, ricotta)
  • Kohuke (Estonia) — ricotta bars covered in chocolate (perfect snack for kids, ~€0.50!). Buy them by the pack!
  • Black bread — the best in the Baltics, lasts for days. Base of every meal
  • Smoked fish — in refrigerated counters, great and cheap (eel, salmon, sprats)

🍳 Van recipes

  • Šaltibarščiai (Lithuanian pink soup) — cooked grated beets + kefir + diced cucumber + dill + boiled egg. Serve cold. No cooking, perfect for summer, kids love the shocking pink color!
  • Baltic tartines — toasted black bread + butter + smoked fish (sprats or salmon) + red onion + dill. No cooking, lunch in 5 minutes.
  • Kefir with berries — fresh kefir (great and cheap in the Baltics) + blueberries/strawberries + honey. Healthy and quick breakfast or snack.

🍽️ To try at the local place

  • Cepelinai (Lithuania) — giant potato dumplings stuffed with meat (huge portions, ~€5)
  • Šaltibarščiai (Lithuania) — cold pink beet soup (kids find it fun!)
  • Piragi (Latvia) — buns filled with bacon and onion
  • Rupjmaizes kārtojums (Latvia) — black bread dessert with whipped cream
  • Verivorst (Estonia) — blood sausage (for the more adventurous)
  • Olde Hansa (Tallinn) — medieval restaurant in the Old Town. Expensive but unique experience (waiters in costume, candles). Kids go crazy! 📍 Maps

💡 Tips

  • The Baltics have prices similar to Poland — take advantage to stock up before Finland
  • Baltic kefir is excellent and very cheap — use it as a drink/breakfast
  • In covered markets you can have lunch with smoked fish + black bread for ~€3-4/person

🇫🇮 Finnish Cuisine (D7-D15)

📅 Itinerary D7-D15 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Finnish cuisine

Finnish cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) is "from forest and lake to table". For centuries Finns lived on fishing, hunting and gathering berries and mushrooms. Kalakukko (fish baked inside a rye bread loaf) is the oldest dish — fishermen carried it as packed lunch. The sauna tradition also influences cuisine: saunapalvikinkku is ham smoked in the sauna for 24 hours! Finns consume more coffee per capita than anyone (12 kg/year!) and more milk than any country. Wild berries (blueberries, arctic cloudberries, lingonberries) are picked by everyone thanks to Jokamiehenoikeus.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Kalakukko — Fish (vendace or perch) baked inside a rye bread crust. Savonia specialty. 📚
  • Karjalanpiirakka — Karelian pasties: rye crust filled with rice porridge, topped with egg butter. National snack! 📚
  • Leipäjuusto — "Bread cheese" — grilled cheese with cloudberry jam (lakka). Squeaky cheese! 📚
  • Lohikeitto — Creamy salmon soup with potatoes and dill. Finnish comfort food par excellence.
  • Mustikkapiirakka — Wild blueberry pie. Finnish blueberries are smaller and more intense than cultivated ones.

🌭 Street food / kiosks / take-away

  • Kauppahalli (covered markets) — every Finnish city has one. Helsinki, Oulu, Rovaniemi. Fresh fish, salmon soups, desserts. Lunch ~€10-12/person. 📍 Oulu Market Hall
  • Karjalanpiirakka — Karelian rice pies with egg butter (typical breakfast, ~€1-2 each, sold everywhere)
  • Grillimakkara — giant grilled sausage (in every picnic area and kiosk, ~€4-5)

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • S-Market/K-Market — prices 30-40% higher than Italy. Excellent fresh salmon (~€12-15/kg). Excellent rye bread
  • Fazer — the blue milk chocolate is iconic (buy as travel snack, ~€2-3)
  • Wild berries — free in the forests! Jokamiehenoikeus (everyman's right) allows picking blueberries, raspberries and mushrooms ANYWHERE, even on private land

🍳 Van recipes

  • Lohikeitto (salmon soup) — fresh salmon cubes + potatoes + carrots + cream + dill. Boil potatoes/carrots 10 min, add salmon 5 min, cream and dill. The perfect dish for cool evenings in Lapland!
  • Pannukakku (Finnish oven pancake) — 3 eggs + 500ml milk + 150g flour + 30g melted butter + 2 tablespoons sugar. Pour into greased pan, cover and cook on medium-low heat 15 min. Serve with jam and cream. Sunday breakfast!
  • Nordic salmon pasta — fresh salmon cubes + cream + dill + lemon + short pasta. The wildcard dish that works all over Scandinavia. 15 min.

🔥 Grill

  • Picnic areas/Laavu — covered structures with fireplaces in the woods are free and everywhere. Bring grillimakkara (sausages) and bread
  • Grillimakkara on fire — Finnish tradition: giant sausage on a stick, grilled over the laavu fire. Kids love the ritual
  • Firewood — in national parks and recreational areas managed by Metsähallitus firewood is provided free (piles next to the structure). Just bring fire starters/matches!

🍽️ To try at the local place

  • Kalakukko — bread filled with fish (specialty of Kuopio)
  • Mustikkapiirakka — blueberry pie (pick blueberries for free in the woods!)
  • Lohikeitto — creamy salmon soup (found in every restaurant/kauppahalli)

💡 Tips

  • Berry picking is a perfect activity for kids — "blueberry hunting" in the woods!
  • Filter coffee is free in many shops and service stations (Finns drink the most coffee per capita in the world!)
  • Finnish picnic areas almost always have free public grills (laavu/kota) — buy sausages and grill!
  • Taps water is excellent everywhere

🇳🇴 Norwegian Cuisine (D15-D35)

📅 Itinerary D15-D35 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Norwegian cuisine

Norwegian cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) is shaped by fjords and extreme climate. Vikings invented preservation: dried fish (tørrfisk/stockfish), smoked (røkelaks) and fermented (rakfisk). Stockfish (📚 Wikipedia) dried in the Lofoten Islands was Viking "currency" — exported across Europe (including Italy for baccalà!). Brunost (brown cheese) was invented in 1863 by a farm girl, Anne Hov, who had the idea of adding cream to whey. Today it's the national cheese. Norwegian salmon is the world's most exported fish — salmon sushi was "invented" by Norwegians who convinced the Japanese in the 1980s!

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Brunost — Sweet brown whey cheese. Cut in thin slices with the ostehøvel (cheese slicer, a Norwegian invention!). 📚
  • Fårikål — Lamb and cabbage stew. Norwegian national dish. Simple but delicious. 📚
  • Rømmegrøt — Sour cream porridge with melted butter, sugar and cinnamon. Mountain farm tradition.
  • Svele — Norwegian pancakes served on ferries! With butter and sugar. Ferry tradition.
  • King Crab (Kongekrabbe) — Giant crab from the north (legs up to 1.5m!). Eaten fresh in Tromsø and Finnmark. 📚

🌭 Street food / kiosks / take-away

  • Svele — Norwegian pancake (sold on ferries, ~NOK 30 (~€2.8)). The quintessential "ferry snack"
  • Bakeri (bakeries) — kanelboller (cinnamon buns) and skillingsboller, perfect snack (~NOK 35-45 (~€3.2–€4.2))
  • Ferries — Svele + coffee at reasonable prices. Every crossing = snack!

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Brunost — sweet brown cheese (caramel flavor, kids love it on bread!)
  • Fiskekaker — fish cakes (easy to heat, ~NOK 40)
  • Røkelaks — smoked salmon (top quality, ~NOK 80-120 (~€7.4–€11))
  • Fresh salmon (~NOK 150-200/kg (~€14–€19)) — superior quality to any Italian restaurant. Cook in the van!
  • Rema 1000 "Middag for en hundrings" — ready meals for 4 at NOK 100 (~€9.3) (special series)
  • Rema 1000/Kiwi — the cheapest supermarkets. Avoid Joker and Meny (expensive)

🍳 Van recipes

  • Nordic salmon — salmon fillet with salt/pepper/dill, cook in pan 4 min per side. Serve with boiled potatoes and pickled cucumber. The dish you will eat most often!
  • Fiskekaker in pan — fish cakes (from supermarket) heated in pan with butter + salad + bread. Dinner in 5 minutes, kids like it.
  • Salmon and cream pasta — fresh salmon cubes + cream + dill + pasta. The Norwegian wildcard dish. 15 min.
  • Fiskesuppe (fish soup) — diced cod/salmon + potatoes + carrots + cream + mild curry. Similar to Lohikeitto but with curry. Perfect after a rainy day.

🔥 Grill

  • Grillhytte — free barbecue huts in many campsites and rest areas. Bring sausages and bread!
  • Firewood — not always provided. Often available at campsites; in remote areas bring charcoal/briquettes or buy a bag of firewood at the gas station (~NOK 50-80). Always keep a supply in the van!

🍽️ To try at the local spot

  • Raspeball/Komle — potato dumplings (west coast, traditional Thursday dish)
  • Vafler — heart-shaped waffles with sour cream and jam (in every café)
  • Bacalao — stockfish (Lofoten = world capital!)
  • Krabbe — king crab (if you find a fisherman, unbeatable price)
  • Fiskebrygga (fishermen's piers) — in Tromsø, Bergen, Svolvær. Fish soup ~NOK 120-150 (~€11–€14)

💡 Tips

  • ⚠️ A restaurant meal for 4 in Norway easily costs €80-120. Cooking in the van is almost mandatory!
  • "10 for 100 kr" offers on fruit and vegetables at Kiwi and Rema 1000
  • Tap water excellent everywhere — often better than bottled water
  • On ferries they sell Svele and coffee — it's the travel snack
  • Bring pasta and rice from Italy (or buy in Poland) — costs double here

🇩🇰 Danish Cuisine (D36-D40)

📅 Itinerary D36-D40 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Danish cuisine

Danish cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) has undergone a revolution: from traditional smørrebrød (open sandwiches) to René Redzepi's "New Nordic" (Noma, 4-time world's best restaurant). Smørrebrød (📚 Wikipedia) originated in medieval times: rye bread was the "plate" on which food was placed. Danish pastries (wienerbrød) were actually brought by Austrian bakers in 1850 during a Danish bakers' strike! Carlsberg was founded in 1847 and its laboratory discovered pure yeast for lager beer — giving the discovery to the world for free.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Smørrebrød — Open sandwiches on rye bread: pickled herring, roast beef, shrimp eggs. A culinary art! 📚
  • Frikadeller — Danish meatballs of pork and veal, fried in butter. With potatoes and parsley sauce. 📚
  • Flæskesteg — Roast pork with crispy crackling. Christmas dish but available year-round in sandwiches (flæskesteg sandwich).
  • Rødgrød med fløde — Red berry compote with cream. The name is impossible to pronounce for non-Danes (Danish test!).
  • Pølsevogn — Danish hot dog cart. The "ristet" (grilled) with remoulade, fried and crispy onions is the classic.

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Pølse — Danish hot dog from red stalls (with pickles and fried onions, ~DKK 40 (~€5.4)). The "ristet hotdog med det hele" is the full version
  • Pølsevogn (red stalls) — the cheapest lunch option for families (~DKK 160 (~€21) for 4 hot dogs)
  • Torvehallerne (Copenhagen) — the nicest food hall. Gourmet Smørrebrød, fresh juices, chocolate. Lunch ~DKK 100-150 (~€13–€20)/person. 📍 Maps
  • Grød — gourmet porridge chain (modern Danish breakfast, ~DKK 65 (~€8.7)). Also savory versions!
  • Softice — soft serve ice cream sold everywhere in summer (~DKK 30 (~€4)) — kids will ask for it every day!

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Netto/Lidl/Rema 1000 — prices 40-50% higher than Italy but less than Norway
  • Rugbrød — dark rye bread (base of every Danish meal, lasts 5-7 days). Always buy some!
  • Lurpak — the most famous Danish butter in the world (excellent and relatively cheap here)
  • Lagkagehuset — the most beloved bakery. Croissants, kanelsnegle (cinnamon rolls), fresh rugbrød

🍳 Van recipes

  • DIY Smørrebrød — rugbrød + butter + smoked salmon (or eggs/cheese/ham) + dill + lemon. No cooking, perfect lunch in 5 minutes!
  • Æbleskiver in pan — 250g flour + 2 eggs + 300ml buttermilk + 1 tsp baking soda + sugar. Cook like small round pancakes in a pan with butter. Serve with powdered sugar and jam. The quintessential Danish snack!
  • Rugbrød with scrambled eggs — toasted rye bread + creamy scrambled eggs + chives + smoked salmon. Danish breakfast in 5 min.

🍽️ To try at the local spot

  • Smørrebrød — open sandwiches with elaborate toppings (try at Torvehallerne)
  • Æbleskiver — spherical pancakes with powdered sugar (kids go crazy for them!)
  • Flæskesteg — roast pork with crispy crackling (the national dish)

💡 Tips

  • At Legoland Billund bring a packed lunch — internal restaurants are very expensive
  • In Denmark dinner is early (18:00-19:00) — perfect times for kids!
  • Rugbrød is the base of everything — always buy a supply
  • Pølsevogn (red stalls) are everywhere and are the cheapest option for families

🇩🇪 German Cuisine (D40 — Bremen transit)

📅 Itinerary D41 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of German cuisine

German cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) varies enormously by region. Bremen, a Hanseatic city, has a maritime tradition: herring, smoked eel, Labskaus (sailors' stew). The Reinheitsgebot (📚 Wikipedia) of 1516 is the world's oldest beer purity law: only water, malt, hops and yeast. Germany has 1,500+ breweries and 5,000+ different beers! Brot (German bread) is UNESCO heritage: over 3,000 registered varieties, more than any other country.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Labskaus (Bremen) — Pink stew of corned beef, beetroot, potatoes with fried egg and herring. Hanseatic sailors' dish! 📚
  • Bremer Knipp — Oat and spiced meat sausage, fried and served with pickles. Bremen local specialty.
  • Franzbrötchen (Hamburg/Bremen) — Cinnamon and butter pastry. The German croissant! Perfect for breakfast.
  • Fischbrötchen — Roll with pickled herring (Matjes), onion and gherkin. Northern street food! 📚

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Currywurst — sliced sausage with curry ketchup sauce and fries. The national fast food, invented in Berlin in 1949. Kids love it! (~€4-5)
  • Brezel (Pretzel) — knot-shaped bread, crunchy outside and soft inside, sprinkled with coarse salt. Perfect travel snack (~€1-2)
  • Bratwurst — grilled sausage in a bun (Brötchen). Each region has its own variant (~€3-4)
  • Fischbrötchen — fish sandwich (marinated herring, salmon or shrimp) typical of the north. At Bremen port ~€4-5

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Aldi/Lidl/Rewe — some of the lowest prices in Western Europe. Excellent fresh bread (Vollkornbrot = whole rye)
  • Kartoffelsalat — ready potato salad (north version: with mayonnaise; south: with vinegar and oil)
  • Apfelschorle — apple juice with sparkling water, the national drink (~€1)
  • Beer — great at ~€0.80. Endless varieties

🍳 Van recipes

  • Currywurst express — sausages (Bratwurst) grilled in a pan, sliced. Sauce: ketchup + curry powder + paprika. Serve with bread or fries. 10 minutes, loved by all!
  • DIY Fischbrötchen — fresh buns + marinated herrings (from the fridge counter) + onion + pickles. No cooking, lunch in 3 minutes.
  • Kartoffelsuppe — diced potatoes + broth + sliced sausages + cream + chives. Hearty soup in 20 min.

🍽️ To try at the local spot

  • Bremer Knipp — Bremen specialty: oat and meat patty, fried in butter (if you find a traditional place)

💡 Tips

  • Bremen is just a transit (D40) — stock up at the German supermarket for the following days in France. German prices are much lower!
  • German bread is the best in Europe — buy Vollkornbrot (lasts days) for the trip

🇫🇷 French Cuisine (D40-D43, D51-D52)

📅 Itinerary D41-D43, D51-D52 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of French cuisine

French cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) has been UNESCO heritage since 2010. Auguste Escoffier (📚 Wikipedia) codified modern cuisine in 1903 with "Le Guide Culinaire". The baguette (📚 Wikipedia) became UNESCO heritage in 2022 — the French consume 10 billion per year! The fromage (cheese) tradition counts 1,200+ varieties — De Gaulle said: "How can you govern a country with 246 different cheeses?". The croissant is actually Austrian (Kipferl), brought to Paris in 1838. Brittany and Normandy (on your route) are famous for crêpes, cider, salted butter and oysters.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Breton crêpes — Buckwheat galettes (savoury) and wheat crêpes (sweet). With cider! 📚
  • Moules-frites — Mussels in white wine with fries. Classic Atlantic coast dish. 📚
  • Camembert — Soft cheese from Normandy. The real one is "au lait cru" (raw milk). 📚
  • Kouign-amann — Breton pastry of butter, butter and more butter. Crispy outside, soft inside. Paradise!
  • Cidre — Breton/Norman apple cider. Sweet (doux) or dry (brut). Drunk from "bolées" (ceramic cups). 📚

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Baguette + fromage — the perfect combo (fresh baguette every morning in boulangerie, ~€1.22!)
  • Marchés (markets) — every city has at least one, 2-3 times a week. Fruit, cheeses, rotisserie (roast chicken ~€8 whole!), olives, bread. The perfect lunch
  • Boulangeries — fresh baguette (€1.22) + cheese + ham = lunch for 4 under €10
  • Crêperies — galette complète (ham/cheese/egg) ~€7-9. Also take-away!

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Intermarché/Leclerc — the cheapest (better than Carrefour). Extraordinary cheeses at €2-4, fresh baguette, excellent wine at €3-5
  • Quiche Lorraine — ready savory tart (~€3-4 per portion)
  • Fromage de chèvre — goat cheeses from the Loire are among the best in the world (Sainte-Maure, Valençay, Crottin de Chavignol, ~€2-4)
  • Loire wines — Vouvray (sweet white), Chinon (light red), Crémant de Loire (sparkling, €5-8!)
  • Pain au chocolat — for kids' breakfast (~€1.22)

🍳 Van recipes

  • Croque-monsieur express — sliced bread + ham + gruyère + béchamel (or just butter). In pan with lid 3 min per side. Dinner in 10 minutes, adored by kids!
  • Van salade niçoise — canned tuna + boiled eggs + tomatoes + olives + green beans + potatoes. No cooking (except eggs), fresh and complete lunch.
  • Ratatouille express — zucchini + eggplant + peppers + diced tomatoes, all in pan with oil and garlic, 20 min on medium heat. Serve with baguette. The dish that tastes of Provence!
  • Tartine au chèvre — baguette + goat cheese + honey + walnuts. Aperitif/light dinner in 2 minutes.

🍽️ To try at the local spot

  • Crêpes — both sweet (Nutella, sugar-lemon) and savory (galettes)
  • Moules-frites — mussels and fries (Atlantic coast)
  • Rillettes de Tours — spreadable pork cream (Loire area, on bread = perfect aperitif)
  • Tarte Tatin — upside-down apple tart (invented by mistake in Lamotte-Beuvron in the Loire!)

💡 Tips

  • France is the motorhome cook's paradise — cheese, bread, wine at unbeatable prices
  • Buy wine directly from the caves (cellars) along the Loire — free tasting and unbeatable prices
  • The pain au chocolat is called "chocolatine" in the southwest — don't make the mistake!
  • Morning markets have fruit/vegetables/cheese at better prices than supermarkets

🇪🇸 Spanish Cuisine (D44-D50)

📅 Itinerary D44-D50 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Spanish cuisine

Spanish cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) of the north is very different from Mediterranean. The Basque Country has the highest concentration of Michelin stars in the world! Pintxos (📚 Wikipedia) are Basque culinary art: mini gastronomic creations on bread, fixed with a toothpick. You go from bar to bar ("txikiteo"). Asturian cider (📚 Wikipedia) is poured from a metre high ("escanciar") to oxygenate it! The Camino de Santiago created a tradition of "menú del peregrino" (3 courses + wine for ~12€) along the entire north.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Basque pintxos — Mini gastronomic masterpieces. The best: gilda (anchovy+olive+chilli), txangurro (crab), croquetas. San Sebastián is the world capital. 📍 Maps Parte Vieja
  • Fabada asturiana — Stew of giant white beans with chorizo, morcilla and bacon. Spain's heartiest dish! 📚
  • Pulpo a la gallega — Boiled octopus with paprika, oil and coarse salt on wood. Galician specialty. 📚
  • Tarta de Santiago — Almond cake with the Santiago cross in powdered sugar. No flour! 📚
  • Asturian cider — Apple cider poured from height (escanciar). Drunk in one gulp! Sidrerías in Oviedo and Gijón.

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Pintxos — snacks on bread (San Sebastián = world capital! ~€2-3 each). The ritual: enter a bar, take 1-2 pintxos + txakoli, pay, move to the next bar
  • Tortilla española — potato omelette (at the bar ~€2 per portion, perfect for children)
  • Churros con chocolate — for breakfast or snack (~€4 for 4 people!)
  • Croquetas — creamy croquettes (ham or cod, ~€1-2 each)
  • Pintxos bar crawl (San Sebastián, Parte Vieja) — budget: ~€30-40 for 4 people if you do 3-4 bars! 📍 Maps
  • Mercado de la Ribera (Bilbao) — the largest covered market in Europe. Pintxos on the upper floor with river view. 📍 Maps

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Mercadona/Lidl/Dia — prices similar or lower than Italy. Excellent ready meals in the fridge section (tortilla, croquetas, ensaladilla) at €2-4
  • Jamón ibérico — sliced ham (~€3), much cheaper than in Italy
  • Gazpacho — cold soup in a bottle (Alvalle or Mercadona brand, ~€2/liter). Perfect for the motorhome in summer, zero cooking!
  • Fruit — excellent and cheap (peaches, watermelons, melons)

🍳 Motorhome recipes

  • Tortilla española — 4 thinly sliced potatoes, fried in plenty of oil, then 6 beaten eggs. Cook on low heat for 10 min, flip with a plate, another 5 min. Eaten hot or cold — lasts 2 days! The wildcard dish of the trip in Spain
  • Fresh gazpacho — ripe tomatoes + pepper + cucumber + garlic + oil + vinegar + stale bread. Blend everything. Serve cold. Zero cooking, refreshing with 35°C!
  • Pan con tomate — toasted bread + rubbed tomato + oil + salt + jamón. Spanish breakfast in 3 minutes. Kids love it!
  • Motorhome patatas bravas — diced potatoes fried in a pan + brava sauce (ketchup + tabasco + smoked paprika + garlic). Hot tapa in 15 min.

🍽️ To try at the restaurant

  • Pulpo a la gallega — Galician-style octopus (with paprika and oil)
  • Asadores (Castile) — restaurants specialized in wood-fired roasts. Lechazo (lamb) and cochinillo (suckling pig) — huge portions to share
  • Fabada asturiana — bean stew (Asturias)
  • Cachopo — giant breaded cutlet stuffed with ham and cheese (Asturias)
  • Sidra natural — Asturian cider poured from above ("escanciar")

💡 Tips

  • In Spain, dinner is late (9:00 PM+). For children, have a "merienda" (substantial snack at 6:00 PM) with tortilla/churros and then a light dinner
  • The menú del día (fixed lunch) costs €10-14 and includes first course + second course + dessert + drink + bread. The best deal in European dining!
  • Spanish children eat croquetas everywhere — they are the local "meatballs"
  • Mercadona has excellent ready meals in the fridge section — perfect for lazy evenings

🇮🇹 Italian Cuisine — Return (D51-D54)

📅 Itinerary D51-D54 · 🏛️ Culture

📜 History of Ligurian cuisine

Ligurian cuisine (📚 Wikipedia) is "poor cuisine" turned gourmet. Pesto genovese (📚 Wikipedia) descends from Roman "moretum" (garlic pounded with cheese), evolved with Genoese DOP basil — smaller and sweeter than common basil. Focaccia genovese (📚 Wikipedia) is eaten for breakfast dipped in cappuccino! Farinata (📚 Wikipedia) was invented by accident: during a storm, barrels of chickpeas and oil spilled in the hold — the mixture baked by the sun became a delicacy. Genoa was "La Superba" of the Maritime Republics: spices, salted anchovies, and maritime trade shaped the cuisine.

🍽️ More dishes to try

  • Trofie al pesto — Hand-rolled fresh pasta with pesto, potatoes and green beans. THE Genoese dish. 📚
  • Focaccia di Recco — Two ultra-thin layers with melted stracchino inside. IGP certified. Different from classic focaccia! 📚
  • Monterosso anchovies — Salted or fried. The Cinque Terre have been famous for anchovies since the 13th century.
  • Cima alla genovese — Stuffed veal pocket with eggs, peas, pine nuts. Served cold in slices. Festive dish. 📚
  • Sciacchetrà — Passito wine from Cinque Terre. Produced in tiny quantities on terraces overlooking the sea. 📚

🌭 Street food / stalls / take-away

  • Focaccia genovese — thin, oily, crispy outside and soft inside. The best in Italy. In downtown bakeries ~€2-3 per portion
  • Farinata — very thin chickpea flour cake baked in a wood oven. Crispy at the edges, soft in the center. Ligurian specialty for 800 years (~€3-4)
  • Panissa — chickpea flour fritters (cousin of farinata, but fried in sticks)
  • Mercato Orientale MOG (Via XX Settembre 75R) — covered market with street food + fresh products. Lunch for 4: ~€25-30. 📍 Maps
  • Friggitoria Carega (Vico Carega 14R) — mixed fried take-away, cone ~€8

🛒 To buy at the supermarket

  • Pesto alla genovese — the real pesto (DOP basil, pine nuts, Parmesan, Fiore Sardo). Buy an artisanal jar at Mercato Orientale as a gastronomic souvenir!

🍳 Motorhome recipes

  • Trofie al pesto — typical Genoese pasta (short, twisted) with fresh pesto, potatoes, and green beans. The dish that marks the return home! 15 min.
  • Last-minute pesto pasta — any short pasta + fresh pesto from Mercato Orientale + a drizzle of oil. The simplest and best dinner after 50 days of travel.

🍽️ To try at the restaurant

  • Mixed fried seafood — calamari, anchovies, shrimp. At the old port ~€10-12 per portion
  • Antica Sciamadda (Via San Giorgio 14R) — farinata + panissa since 1700! Portions ~€3-5. Institution. 📍 Maps

💡 Tips

  • After 50+ days of foreign cuisines, focaccia genovese will be the best "welcome back to Italy" possible!
  • Trofie al pesto with potatoes and green beans — the quintessential Ligurian dish, not to be missed before going home

📍 Fishing — Details by Area

🇫🇮 Finland (D8–D16)

Finland is a fishing paradise. 188,000 lakes, very clean waters, and a simple licensing system.

What to fish: pike (hauki), perch (ahven), whitefish (siika), lake trout (taimen), Arctic char (nieriä in Lapland).

Tip for kids: fishing with mato-onki (fixed rod with float and worm) is completely free for everyone in Finland, no license needed. Just a 3-4m rod, a float, and worms. Guaranteed perch!

Best spots on the route:

🎣 D8-D9 Lake Saimaa: Finland's largest lake. Pike and perch from any pier.

🎣 D13 Rovaniemi: Kemijoki river (salmon running in July — special local permit required).

🎣 D14 Lake Inari: lake trout and Arctic char. Local permit ~€10/day on eraluvat.fi.

🎣 D15 Kilpisjärvi: Arctic char. Permit ~€8/day at visitor center.

Recommended gear: 2.4m telescopic rod + small reel + 5-10g spinner lures (for pike/perch) + floats and hooks for mato-onki (kids).

🇳🇴 Norway (D16–D35)

Norway is every angler's dream: sea fishing is FREE for everyone, no license, no age limits. And the fjords are full of fish.

What to fish (sea): Arctic cod (torsk), pollack (sei), mackerel (makrell), halibut (kveite), coal cod (lyr).

The 5 must-visit spots:

  1. D17 Senja — Husøy and Mefjordvær piers. Cod and pollack. Midnight sun = fishing at 11:00 PM!

  2. D21 Henningsvær — fishing village, piers everywhere. Arctic cod.

  3. D23 Reine — fishing with red rorbu cabins in the background. Iconic.

  4. D28 Atlantic Road — fishing FROM THE BRIDGES over the ocean. Pollack and cod.

  5. D32 Bergen — Bryggen pier, mackerel in July (seasonal peak).

Rules:

Sea/fjord: FREE, no limits (personal use)

Freshwater (rivers/lakes): license required from owner

Salmon/sea trout: mandatory state fee on elveguiden.no + local license

Export: max 15 kg fish per person from 2026 (only if caught at registered tourist facility; self-guided fishing = 0 kg exportable)

Gear: same as Finland works perfectly. For cod from piers: 50-100g sinker + artificial bait (jig) or mackerel fillet.

🇩🇰 Denmark (D36–D40)

Mandatory license (Fisketegn) but easy to get online.

Spots on the route:

🎣 D36 Hirtshals (if you stop): harbor pier — mackerel, cod. Great with kids.

🎣 D38 Billund area: lakes and rivers — trout, pike. Local lake permit also required.

Purchase: fisketegn.dk/en — DKK 46 (~€6.2)/day, DKK 150 (~€20)/week. Kids <18 free.

🇩🇪 Germany (D40–D41)

⚠️ Not recommended. Germany requires an exam (Fischereischein) to fish. Some Länder offer tourist licenses, but Bremen (on your route) is not among them. Skip fishing here — you will have plenty of opportunities before and after.

🇫🇷 France (D41–D43, D51)

Sea: shore fishing FREE, no license (like Italy).

Freshwater: Carte de pêche (~€15/day) required, purchasable at cartedepeche.fr. Kids <12 free.

Spots:

🎣 D43 Loire: Cher river near Chenonceau — pike, perch. Carte de pêche required.

🎣 D51 French Riviera: piers of Antibes, Cannes, Menton — gilthead bream, seabream. FREE.

🇪🇸 Spain (D44–D50)

Sea: ⚠️ Recreational sea fishing license required in all Autonomous Communities (Basque Country, Cantabria, Catalonia). Cost: ~€43/3 years online at fishinglicensespain.com (covers all Spain). Fines without license: €60-600!

Freshwater: additional regional license required (varies by Autonomous Community, ~€10-15/day online).

Spots:

🎣 D44 San Sebastián: harbor pier or Igueldo cliffs — seabream, mackerel.

🎣 D45 Getxo (near Bilbao): old port — sea bass, seabream.

🎣 D46 Picos de Europa: Deva river — brown trout! Cantabria license ~€15/day at medioambientecantabria.es. Many catch & release areas.

🎣 D50 Costa Brava: Cap de Creus, Cadaqués pier — gilthead bream, sea bass.

🇮🇹 Italy (D52–D54)

Sea: shore fishing FREE, no license required.

Spots:

🎣 D53 Genoa: Boccadasse pier or outer breakwater of Porto Antico — white seabream, seabream, mullet.

⚠️ Logistical Risks — Critical Points

1. "The Baltic Marathon" (D3–D6)

This is the real test of the trip. On paper it seems doable, but in reality it is a stress test for the children:

🚗 D3: Vienna → Warsaw (670 km, 8h 30m)

🚗 D4: Warsaw → Vilnius (510 km, 6h)

🚗 D5: Vilnius → Riga (300 km, 4h)

🚗 D6: Riga ➔ Pärnu ➔ Haapsalu ➔ Tallinn (390 km, 5h)

The problem: You will drive about 1,870 km across 4 driving days (D3–D6), with D7 as a rest day in Tallinn. Considering bathroom breaks, lunches, and traffic, actual travel times will increase by 20-30%. The children will spend most of the time strapped in car seats.

Solution: Always start at dawn (e.g. 5:00 or 6:00 AM). The children will sleep for the first 3-4 hours of travel, allowing you to "cover kilometers" peacefully and arrive at the destination by lunchtime, saving afternoons for parks and explorations.

2. Leaving the Lofoten (D25: Lofoten → Mo i Rana)

The table shows 400 km, 5h + ferry. Be careful as this estimate is optimistic:

The Moskenes–Bodø ferry lasts about 3.5–4 hours

You must be at the boarding at least 1 hour (or 2) before

From Bodø to Mo i Rana there are about 230 km (another 3.5 hours driving)

The problem: This will be a draining day, almost exclusively transit (about 9-10 hours total including driving, waiting, and sailing). Consider it a "lost" day tourist-wise and prepare onboard entertainment for the children.

3. The Ferry Uncertainty (Mandatory Reservations)

The trip relies on some crucial ferries (Tallinn–Helsinki, Moskenes–Bodø, Kristiansand–Hirtshals).

In August and late July, the Moskenes–Bodø route is overwhelmed by motorhome travelers from all over Europe. If you show up at boarding without a reservation, you might have to wait 24 or 48 hours in line (in the "standby lane").

⚠️ You absolutely must book these ferries 2-3 months in advance, which will bind you to strictly follow the schedule up to D25.

The Kristiansand–Hirtshals ferry (D36) is less critical but booking is still recommended in high season.

4. Ferragosto in Genoa (D51–D53)

As noted in the file, the A10 highway at Ferragosto is hell. Being in Genoa on 17/08 (D53) saves you from the absolute peak, but expect very heavy slowdowns in Liguria.

Tip: On 16/08 (D52) from Camargue to Genoa (450 km, A8/A10) — leave at 7:00 AM to avoid the bulk of return traffic. Consider stopping in Savona/Finale Ligure if traffic is blocked.

iPhone: The app must be installed to the Home Screen ("Add to Home Screen" from Safari). Push works only on iOS 16.4+.

              
Desktop: Check that the browser isn't blocking notifications for this site (lock icon in the URL bar).
